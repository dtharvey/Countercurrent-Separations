On the previous tab we visualized the progress of a countercurrent extraction as both a numerical value and as the shading of a square that represents a tube. Although effective, this visualization is limited by the space needed to display steps and tubes, and is limited to a single solute.

The figure on the right is a more compact way to display a countercurrent extraction—in this case for two solutes—using up to \(100\) tubes and \(100\) steps. Sliders define the fraction of each solute, \(p_a\) and \(p_b\), that moves from the stationary phase into the mobile phase. A third slider displays the contents of each tube after the designated number of steps; a play button on this slider animates the separation.

Exercise 1. The default settings are \(p_a = 0.83\) and \(p_b = 0.33\) for solute B, and 50 steps. Adjust the slider for steps to 10 and describe the distribution of each solute giving attention to position, shape, height, and width. Repeat for steps of \(20\), \(30\), \(40\), \(50\), \(60\), \(70\), \(80\), \(90\), and \(100\). Is there a relationship between p, the number of steps, and the position of the solute’s distribution?

Exercise 2. Using the default conditions of \(p_a = 0.83\) and \(p_b = 0.33\), determine the fewest number of steps that will allow for an effective separation of the two solutes. Give some thought to how you will define an effective separation.

Exercise 3. Use the slider for solute A and determine the largest value of \(p_a\) that will allow for an effective separation of the two solutes after \(100\) steps if \(p_b = 0.33\) for solute B. Repeat for solute B, determining the smallest value of \(p_b\) that will allow for an effective separation of the two solutes after \(100\) steps if \(p_a = 0.83\) for solute A.

Exercise 4. Given the limitation of \(100\) steps, is it possible to achieve a complete separation of two solutes if one solute has \(p = 0.2\) and the other solute has \(p = 0.25\)?
